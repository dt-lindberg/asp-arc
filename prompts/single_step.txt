<encoding_strategies>
Choose the strategy that best fits the puzzle:

STRATEGY A — Direct derivation (use when each output cell is uniquely determined by the rule):
  output(R,C,new_col) :- input(R,C,old_col), maps(old_col, new_col).
  has_color(R,C) :- output(R,C,V), V != 0.
  output(R,C,0) :- output_cell(R,C), not has_color(R,C).    % fill background
  :- output(R,C,V1), output(R,C,V2), V1 != V2.              % uniqueness

STRATEGY B — Choice + Constraints (use when you need to eliminate wrong candidates):
  1 { output(R,C,Col) : color(Col) } 1 :- output_cell(R,C).
  :- [conditions that eliminate wrong colorings]

For most ARC puzzles, Strategy A is cleaner when the rule directly maps input to output.
</encoding_strategies>

<worked_example>
Puzzle: apply a fixed color substitution (color 3→4, 1→5, 2→6, everything else unchanged).

```asp
% --- Color substitution mapping ---
maps(1,5). maps(2,6). maps(3,4). maps(4,3). maps(5,1). maps(6,2).

% --- Grid dimensions (same as input) ---
height(H) :- H = 1 + #max { R : input(R,_,_) }.
width(W)  :- W = 1 + #max { C : input(_,C,_) }.
output_cell(R,C) :- height(H), width(W), R = 0..H-1, C = 0..W-1.

% --- Direct derivation: apply mapping where defined, else keep original color ---
output(R,C,New) :- input(R,C,Old), maps(Old, New).
output(R,C,V)   :- input(R,C,V), not maps(V, _).

% --- Uniqueness constraint ---
:- output(R,C,V1), output(R,C,V2), V1 != V2.
```
